Following all the guidelines talked about in class and the methods learned in previous classes, you are required to re-design the Google Classroom Logo, incorporating a symbol. The symbol needs to represent this education app. The symbol and logotype should be designed to be one element, not a pictogram next to the words or letters, for example. Think about the positive and negative spaces created within and around letters, and how these may be utilised to create a symbol of some sort. The logo must be created in an Adobe software program – Illustrator or Photoshop.
In the brief of this class task, I was tasked with redesigning the Google Classroom logo to represent the app as an educational platform. It was to be designed as a single element with consideration to the graphic design principles and elements and incorporate a distinguishable symbol. I believe my final solution meets this criteria and also includes other branding related aspects that further improve it.

To ensure this design is apt at all sizes, I used only thick lines, minimal detail and strong contrast. I aligned and scaled the books to balance the logo and unify it into a square shape. Both the G and C I vertically centred and aligned with the curves of the blue book, increasing the formality of the logo. I also used common shapes and avoided using any textures or artistic esque style to further its formality as a performing software platform. To identify google classroom as educational, I used 3 stacked books in this logo. Not only is this a common symbol for education, but it is also a universal symbol, that is, it is recognizable across the world. It is also simple and identifiable at all sizes. Furthermore, to ensure this is a modern logo I’ve complied with the conventional flat UI design principle and used pure, brightly contrasting colours.

An interesting flaw in the current Google Classroom logo is that it doesn’t use colours from the super identifiable google colour palette, unlike every other google logo. I assume this was done because otherwise the symbol of the logo would be hard to identify as a school blackboard. On the other hand, books are not bound by colour and so I was able to incorporate the google colours while still maintaining the recognisability of the symbol. Resultantly my logo now fits nicely into the suite of google logos and is immediately identifiable as a google logo.

Overall, I think my logo complies well with the design principles and elements, uses effective symbolism, and identifies well as a google logo. I am somewhat uncertain as to whether the book symbols are immediately distinguishable as books. I also think that in situations where a large version of the logo is used, it would benefit from having accompanying text saying ‘Google Classroom’ underneath. This would help people who are viewing the logo for the first time with defining it, and could considerably improve communication when, for example, the product is launching in a new area. Beyond these I can’t see any flaws in the execution of this design.

TASK 6 T-SHIRT DESIGN
You are required to design a T-Shirt graphic for people aged 16-21. Your target market is your peers, so you will need to find out what they want. Do they want bright or dark colours? What styles do they like? Should there be a theme? What message do they want to send?

You need to follow the design process (brief, research, concepts, development, solution, evaluation, presentation), and will need to use Adobe Illustrator to produce your final design. There should be no raster graphics in your design, all elements must be vector.

Not one person I asked had specific preferences for colours, themes or designs that highlighted a specific design principle. The target audience is the age range where everyone is establishing their personal identities. It is also the age range where people are attempting to care less about what their peers think about them yet at the same time care a lot about whether their peers think they care. Hence a lot of people between the ages of 16 and 21 are focused on developing a unique, often edgy fashion style. This explains why the overwhelmingly dominant preference people had for Tshirt designs were for them to be original and unique. I’ve also noticed that people have a greater inkling towards unique illustration styles and illustrated characters.
The brief for this task was to design a T-Shirt popular amongst my peers. To achieve this I established I would need to create a T-Shirt that compiled together an original exploration of design elements and ideas using both graphic and illustrative techniques. I also established that these explorations would need to come to some sort of finite conclusions so that the design doesn’t end out feeling lost.

My first idea for the design was to explore hand drawn typography styles. I tried to overlay as many styles as possible, exploring the ways in which they can be distorted and warped yet still legible. I also layered letters in different styles and used lines and shapes to accent them. I did this simply because I haven’t seen it done before, beyond the work of David Carson. I then went on to compliment these letters with a handful of abstract shapes and patterns, which I used to tinker with depth and transparency.

To compliment the laid back and casual air of the illustrations I coloured the design using a varied autumn colour palette. In particular I used some dim cool colours that transition into some dull pinks and yellows (plus some fluro green). This adds to the sporadic style of the illustration, but so that it isn’t too scattered I clustered similar colours to help distinguish each letter.

I thought to help balance the design, it required an element of scale, so I added the central illustration. I scaled it up to have thick strokes and contain more negative space. This now serves as the central and initial focus of the design. I placed it in a rectangle to add some much needed negative space. Doing this also added some structure by segregating the illustrations into chunks. The white fill of the central illustration and dull monotone background colour contrast nicely with the more chaotic elements above and below. I further boxed all of these elements together with a rectangle in an attempt to unify the design.

As some final touches I added some graphical boxes and typography to the top left whose alignment and cleanness contrast nicely. I lacked any inspiration for meaningful text and wanted to refrain from using random words (a choice I somewhat regret when drafting the initial illustration of capital), so I used the water ratings url for because it adds a humorous element to the design and makes it somewhat edgier.

I wonder if because I have slowly developed each of the elements in this design, I have become accustomed to them and as a result the design seems more familiar and simpler to me than to others. I fear that it may seem too chaotic and complex to others, Its exploration of so many ideas simultaneously may make it appear lost. I think that this design would do better if it existed as a later development, a new arrangement of illustrations and ideas already familiar. That way, my peers would be able to explore each idea sequentially and methodically, the way I did, thus making the final design more sensicle. I would also have liked to improve the alignment of this design because I feel that its balance between chaos and structure is not quite level.

In future I think there is still much room for exploration with the elements of this design. One area I omitted exploring was texture, some hard sandpaper esque textures could have really helped solidify this design. I would like to trail different arrangements and alignments and also explore some visual ideas like masks and windows, which could help simplify the design and make it more proportional. However overall I consider this design to be successful and confidently think it matches the brief.

TASK 8 DRAMA POSTER
In the brief for this task, I was tasked with creating a poster for the school production. I wanted the poster to capture the lighthearted and cheerful nature of the play and of course include some elements which hint at what it’s about.

Drawing from the other spelling bee posters, I considered a stage and a microphone to be the best way to reflect the play and as it’s a play about words, I thought books would be a good complement. I further included a spotlight as a way of creating depth and visual interest, but also as a symbolic reference to the theatrical intensity and pressure of a solo performance.

The play seems to center around strong school based stereotypes. To match this theme I focused on giving the poster a stereotypical casual arts and craft style, a style that would be well supported by the predefined colour palette. Inspired from the other spelling bee posters, I hand drew a custom font for the words ‘Spelling Bee’ that look as though they were drawn with a marker, I textured the purple background such that it appears to be a material, and I added virtual white paint splatters to the microphone. Furthering the amateurish craft style of this poster, I included elements that suggest depth but in unrealistic ways; the books are stacked with isometric depth yet have no effect on the lighting of each other and the microphone placed on top is visibly flat, and there’s apparent light shining on the books and microphone yet there are no lighter tones where the light should be hitting and no shadows are cast. To signify that these are deliberate effects, I contrast them with a folded paper effect with realistic shadows. Finally I used a typewriter font for all of the accompanying text as yet another craft styled element.

Balance and emphasis were two leading priorities I had for this design in order to achieve quick and clear communication. I scaled the words ‘Spelling Bee’ to be the most prominent element of the design, setting up the context for the illustrations, which are of similar emphasis but take up a smaller area. All text (including the text on the back if you follow the isometric path) is aligned along the same margins. To unify the smaller blocks of text, I include blue rectangles in their margins. This also helps guide the users attention from one to the other in a left, right, left fashion that helps provide balance. I used colour to group elements and create contrast in other areas as well. The books and microphone are distinguished by the white surrounds, and ‘Spelling Bee’ is unified with yellow. In their multi colouring, the books also appear more strongly as one group.

The design I have created I think fits the brief aptly, however in reflective hindsight, I see a couple of things that could be improved upon. I opted to not include a character to avoid the risk of over cluttering the poster, however I think using a character instead of the microphone could have been more reflective of the play. On another note, while there is adequate negative space in the poster, I think scaling down the books, microphone and spotlight and increasing negative space would make the poster visually easier to digest. It would also further enhance the depth and distance communicated in the poster. Lastly, I think some subtle texture additions could possibly have improved the poster; adding some streaks along the spotlight and slightly embossing the words ‘Spelling Bee’.
